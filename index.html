Why Delphi Uses DPM

No jargon. No fluff. Just a straight explanation of how Delphi actually works — and why your payout looks the way it does.

If you have ever looked at your potential payout on Delphi and thought "wait, why did this number change?" — this article is for you.

Delphi is not like a normal betting site. There are no fixed odds. No bookmaker setting a price. Instead, Delphi runs on something called DPM — Dynamic Parimutuel Market. Once you understand how it works, a lot of things start making sense.

The Basic Idea: A Shared Pool

Think of every market on Delphi as a shared prize pool. When people put money in, that money goes into the pool. When the market settles, the entire pool gets split among the winners — based on how many shares they hold.

The key formula is simple:

Payout = Your Shares × Settlement Price

That is it. Not your investment multiplied by some percentage. Your shares multiplied by what each share is worth at settlement.

Why Early Entry Matters

Here is where DPM gets interesting. Every time someone buys shares on an outcome, the price goes up automatically. The more people pile in, the more expensive it gets.

So if you enter early, you get more shares for the same amount of money. If you enter late, you get fewer shares because the price has already moved up.

Early buyer → cheaper price → more shares → bigger slice of the pool
Late buyer → expensive price → fewer shares → smaller slice of the pool

Real Example: The GTA Trailer Market

A user entered the "Will GTA Trailer 3 come out before or after May 27th?" market early. At that point, the potential payout was showing around $8.00.

Over time, more people joined and bet on the same side. The pool grew — but so did the number of winners sharing it. By the time the market settled, the claimable amount had dropped to $3.75.

This is not a bug. This is DPM doing exactly what it is designed to do. More winners = smaller individual cut, even if you entered early.

The lesson? If everyone agrees with you, your payout will be lower. The biggest payouts go to people who were right when most others were wrong.

Another Real Example: The Payout Math

Here is an actual calculation from a Delphi market to show how the formula works in practice.

A user invested $20.79 and ended up with 41.87 shares. The settlement price came in at $0.89 (meaning the winning outcome resolved at 88.9%).

Payout = 41.87 shares × $0.89 = $37.27

Some people thought the payout should be much higher — like 200% returns. But that was based on a misunderstanding. The 88.9% settlement price and the investment amount are not directly related. You cannot just multiply them together.

The actual return ratio here was about 1.79x — because the average entry price was around 49.7 cents, very close to 50/50. That is why the return was close to 1.8x and not 2x or more.

What About Low Liquidity Markets?

A common concern on Delphi is seeing a market with low activity and thinking "this market is dead, I will not get my money back."

That is the wrong way to think about it here. Delphi is not an order book like Polymarket where low liquidity means you cannot find a buyer. DPM does not work like that.

In DPM, the cost function handles every trade automatically. You do not need someone on the other side to match your bet. The market will always take your trade. Your position size and payout percentage are determined by your entry price and shares — not by how many other people showed up.

A quiet market on Delphi is not a broken market. It is just a market with fewer participants. Your position is still valid.

The One Real Tradeoff

DPM is not perfect. The main thing to know is that the price in a DPM market is not the same as the probability of an outcome.

On some platforms, if something is priced at 70 cents, that means the crowd thinks there is a 70% chance it happens. On Delphi, it is slightly more complex than that. The implied probability needs a bit of extra calculation under the hood.

For most users this will not matter day to day. But it is worth knowing so you are not surprised.

Quick Summary

Delphi uses DPM because it works well for a new platform with thin markets. It lets anyone trade at any time. It keeps risk manageable for market creators. And it is built on solid academic foundations — not hype.

The tradeoffs are real and the team is open about them. No mechanism is perfect. But for what Delphi is trying to build, DPM is a thoughtful starting point.
