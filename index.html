Prediction Markets

Why Delphi Uses DPM

A simple look at Dynamic Parimutuel Markets, why they help small prediction markets, and what tradeoff they bring.

Prediction markets are used to collect people's views about the future. A market may ask a question like, "Will Bitcoin reach a new high this year?" People can buy shares for Yes or No. As people trade, the market shows what the crowd believes.

But a prediction market needs a good system behind it. If the system is weak, users may not be able to trade easily. Prices may also give poor signals. This is why the market mechanism matters.

What DPM Means

DPM means Dynamic Parimutuel Market. It is a way to run a prediction market.

A normal parimutuel market works like a shared pool. Users put money into the pool. When the event ends, the people who picked the winning side share the pool.

DPM takes this idea and makes it more useful for modern prediction markets. It lets people trade while the market is still open. The market can react when new information comes in.

Why Delphi Chose It

Delphi is still a new platform. Many of its markets may start small. Some markets may have only a few traders at first.

DPM helps in this situation because users can still trade even when a market is thin. They do not always need to wait for another person to take the opposite side.

DPM also helps the person who creates the market. The creator does not have to take unlimited risk. Their maximum loss can be limited. This is important because Delphi wants many people to create markets.

The Cost Function

Delphi uses DPM with a cost function. In simple words, the cost function is the formula that controls the market.

It decides how much a trade costs. It also decides how the market changes after each trade.

This makes the system cleaner. It is easier to build, easier to check, and easier to improve later.

The Main Tradeoff

DPM is useful, but it is not perfect. The main downside is that the price is not always the same as probability.

In many prediction markets, a price of 70 cents may look like a 70% chance. In DPM, it is not that simple. The real probability needs extra calculation.

So Delphi gets strong liquidity and lower risk for market creators. But users must remember that DPM prices are not always direct probabilities.

Why This Matters

Prediction markets should be honest about their limits. No mechanism is perfect. Every design has a tradeoff.

Delphi chose DPM because it fits the needs of a young decentralized prediction market platform. It works well for small markets. It helps users trade. It keeps risk controlled for market creators. And it gives Delphi a strong base to build on.

In short, DPM is a practical starting point. It is not magic. It is a careful choice for the kind of markets Delphi wants to support.
