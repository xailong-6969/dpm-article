If you have ever placed a position on Delphi and wondered why the payout number changed, this will make it easier to read.

Delphi runs on DPM — Dynamic Parimutuel Market. It is not like a traditional betting platform. There are no fixed odds, no bookmaker on the other side. The market prices itself based on what people do. Once you understand the core idea, everything else falls into place.

Before You Trade

The main thing to know is that Delphi does not use fixed odds. You buy shares from a pool, and the pool keeps updating as more users trade.

That is why two users can spend the same amount and still end up with different payouts. Entry price, share count, and pool size all matter.

Quick DPM Read

Think of a Delphi market like a shared prize pool. People buy shares on either side, usually Yes or No.

  • Enter early: shares are usually cheaper, so the same money can buy more shares.
  • More people buy one side: that side gets more expensive, so later buyers receive fewer shares.
  • Your payout can change: it depends on your shares, the final pool size, and how many other winners split that pool.
  • More people on your side: good for confidence, but it can also mean more winners sharing the same pot.

The Core Idea: One Shared Pool

Every market on Delphi is a shared prize pool. People put money in by buying shares on an outcome. When the market settles, the entire pool gets distributed among the winners — based on how many shares each person holds.

The payout comes from this idea:

Payout = Your Shares × Final Payout Per Share

Your shares are determined by when you entered and at what price. The final payout per share is known when the market settles. These two numbers multiplied together give you your final payout.

How the Price Moves

In DPM, the price is not set by anyone. It moves automatically based on demand. Every time someone buys shares on an outcome, the price for that outcome goes up. The more people pile in, the more expensive it gets for the next buyer.

This is by design. Early buyers can get a better entry if they are right, because lower prices can mean more shares before a side gets crowded.

Example 1: Cricket Final

A market opens: "Who will win the final - MI or CSK?"

At the start, both sides are quiet. You believe MI will win and put in $10 early when the price is low — say 30 cents per share. You receive roughly 33 shares.

As the tournament progresses, more people start backing MI. The price climbs to 60 cents, then 80 cents. A late buyer puts in the same $10 but only gets 12 shares.

MI wins. You hold 33 shares, the late buyer holds 12. You take a much bigger cut of the same pool — simply because you committed earlier when the price was cheap.

Example 2: Bitcoin Price Target

A market asks: "Will BTC close above the target price before the deadline?"

Early on, very few people believe it. YES shares are cheap at 20 cents. You buy $20 worth and receive 100 shares.

Later, BTC moves sharply upward. Everyone starts buying YES. Price jumps to 75 cents. A new buyer puts in $20 and gets only 26 shares.

BTC closes above the target. Market resolves YES. Your 100 shares versus their 26 — you walk away with nearly 4× their payout despite investing the same $20.

Example 3: Esports Tournament

Market: "Will Team Liquid win the next major CS2 tournament?"

Most people bet YES early because of hype. YES price shoots to 70 cents. You think NO is underpriced and buy NO shares at 25 cents — getting 40 shares for $10.

Team Liquid loses in the semis. Market resolves NO. Almost nobody was on your side — so the pool is large and spread among very few winners. Your 40 NO shares claim a massive fraction of the total pool.

This is the contrarian advantage in DPM. Being right when everyone else was wrong pays the most.

Example 4: GTA VI Trailer

A real one. Market: "Will GTA Trailer 3 release before or after May 27th?"

A user entered early when the potential payout showed around $8.00. But over time, more and more people joined the same side.

The pool grew, but the number of winners grew too. Their share of the winning pool became smaller, so the final claim dropped to $3.75.

That is not a bug. When many people are right with you, the winning side has to split the pot between more people.

How Liquidity Works in DPM

This is the part most people get wrong — especially those coming from platforms like Polymarket.

On an order book platform, low liquidity means you might not find a buyer or seller on the other side. That is a real concern there. DPM does not work like that.

Think of it like a DEX liquidity pool. You do not need a matching buyer in the same order-book sense, but trades are still subject to liquidity, slippage, and market status. DPM is the same idea applied to prediction markets.

What Happens if You Pick the Wrong Outcome?

Simple answer — if you hold shares on the losing outcome at settlement, that position is worth $0. The entire pool goes to the winners.

DPM does not change the basic rule: wrong side gets $0. The DPM part is how entry price, shares, and final payout are calculated.

This is why position sizing matters. Only put in what you are comfortable losing if the outcome goes against you.

One Thing to Keep in Mind

DPM prices and probabilities are not the same number. On some fixed-payout prediction markets, a price of 70 cents is often read as about a 70% chance. In DPM, you should not read the price that directly. The implied probability requires an extra calculation under the hood.

For most day-to-day usage this will not affect you. But it is worth knowing so you are not caught off guard when comparing prices across platforms.

Delphi Payout FAQ: How DPM Works

Q: What is DPM?

DPM means Dynamic Parimutuel Market.

In simple words, Delphi markets use a pool-based payout system. Traders buy positions on different outcomes, and the money goes into the market pool. When the market settles, the winning side gets paid from that pool.

DPM = decides how the pool is shared between winners

Q: Is Delphi payout fixed like “1 winning share = $1”?

No. Delphi payout is not fixed like that. In a DPM market, your final payout depends on:

The total market pool How many winning shares exist How much liquidity the market has What price you entered at What other traders do before the market closes Fees / market rules

So your estimated payout can change while the market is still live.

Q: Where does the payout come from?

The payout comes from the market pool. When users buy the provided outcome, that money becomes part of the market’s pool. After settlement, the winning outcome holders share the available pool.

Simple version: Losers’ money helps pay winners.

That is why it is called parimutuel. Traders are not betting against Delphi as a house. Traders are effectively trading against the rest of the market pool.

Q: Simple payout example?

Let’s say there is a market: Will Argentina win the match?

Users buy both sides:

YES buyers put money into YES NO buyers put money into NO

Now imagine the total market pool is $1,000. If YES wins, then YES holders share the pool. If there are 500 winning YES shares, then the rough payout is:

$1,000 ÷ 500 = $2 per YES share

So if you hold 50 YES shares, your payout would be around:

50 × $2 = $100

This is just a simple example.

Q: Why does my estimated payout change?

Because the market is still active. Your payout estimate can change when:

More people buy your side More people buy the opposite side Liquidity changes the price impact Someone sells their position The pool grows before market close

For example, if many people buy the same winning side, the pool is shared among more winning shares. That can reduce payout per share. But if many people buy the losing side, that can increase the pool available to the winners.

So more volume alone does not always mean higher payout for you. It depends where that volume goes.

Q: What does price mean in DPM?

The price is like the market’s current implied belief, but it is also affected by liquidity and trading activity.

If YES is showing around 70%, it means the market is pricing YES as the stronger side at that moment. But traders should understand this clearly:

Price is not a guarantee.

It is just the current market price based on trading activity.

In low-liquidity markets, price can move sharply even from small trades, so it may not always reflect true crowd confidence.

Q: Is high volume always good for payout?

Not always.

Volume only means trading activity. It does not automatically mean your payout will be higher.

Example:

If you bought YES and most later volume also buys YES, then more winning shares may be created. If YES wins, the pool has to be shared between more YES holders.

But if you bought YES and many users buy NO, then if YES wins, there may be more losing-side money in the pool for YES holders.

So traders should not only look at volume. They should also look at:

Which side is receiving volume Current price Liquidity Estimated payout Entry timing Market rules

Q: Does low liquidity affect payout?

Yes, indirectly.

Low liquidity does not mean the market cannot pay winners. The DPM design is pool-based, so payouts come from the pool.

low liquidity mainly affects price quality, slippage, trade impact, and whether the market is currently available for trading.

What To Remember

DPM is a shared pool system. Prices move automatically based on demand. Early entry can help if it gives you more shares at a better price, but winners still split the pool based on shares held — not fixed odds.

Low liquidity does not mean your position is broken — it usually means a smaller pool with more price impact. Strong payouts often come from being right when most others were wrong.

For Delphi users, the main idea is simple: your outcome matters, but your entry price and share count matter too.

If a payout looks different later, it usually means the pool changed after more users traded.
